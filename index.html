Ada Lovelace

Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace, was born in London in 1815, better known as Ada Lovelace, was the first person to write an algorithm to be processed by a machine in the history of computing.

Childhood

His father was the romantic poet Lord Byron, known for his exaggerations such as having a bear domesticated in his room and by acts (unusual for the time), such as relating to men and women. His mother was the rich and cultured Annabella Milbanke, in love with mathematics and not very patient with her husband's craziness.

Lord Byron was a heavy drinker which resulted in their divorce when Ada was only 5 weeks old. Annabella was a single mother concerned with ensuring that Ada did not look like her father. He invested in the girl's rigorous education by hiring the best teachers. believed that if her daughter were interested in math and science, she would stay away from poetry and eccentric tendencies inherited from the father.

Ada was fascinated by mathematics and poetry (much to her mother's dismay). At twelve years old, became enchanted by mechanical engineering and wrote a book called Flyology (or Voologia), with drawings of a plan for the construction of a flying machine. When telling your mother about the machine, Annabelle thought that Ada was deviating from her studies and reprimanded her. So he asked Ada to focus on becoming a lady.

Youth

At age 17, Ada was introduced to the court and began to be invited to parties in the city. Her smart and quick manner attracted many singles, but she became interested in an old man. mathematician named Charles Babage, who told him about the invention he had been working on for years, the “differential machine”, which made large calculations.

Charles liked the young woman, she was one of the few people who was interested and understood his inventions. When Ada got to know the differential machine, she saw in it many possibilities that not even Charles had seen. thought. The enthusiasm of the young mathematician impressed him.

Adulthood

Charles was a widower and struggled for investments for his projects. Ada got married and had 3 sons. In 1830, this meant that she had to abandon her studies and dedicate herself to the sons. I exchanged letters with Charles, followed his work from afar. had little time for your own studies. He started to solve mathematical problems at night, after children sleep. What she wanted was to work with Charles on his amazing machines.

Later, Charles created the Analytical Engine, better than the differential machine, because it would be able to analyze data and store information in memory. something so revolutionary that few people understood. To publicize your new machine, you would need to publish articles in French, a language he didn't know, but Ada was fluent. As she understood Charles' creations, he was the perfect person to translate these articles.

While Ada translated the content, took notes on her own, imagined countless possibilities that the machine would have. It didn't take long for his notes to get bigger than than the article itself.

Charles wanted the machine to solve mathematical problems. Ada was thinking about something a lot bigger: the numbers could represent totally different things like letters and grades musicals. He wrote detailed instructions with diagrams and tables explaining how the device analytics should process a complicated mathematical equation. Thus, the machine would solve in seconds what a human would take hours to solve. Computers are not yet existed, but Ada had just created the first computer program. ada did not live to see that his ideas would turn into the modern computers we use today.
